# Independent (Lay) Witnesses

Reference guidance for correspondence with non-party, non-expert witnesses who have factual knowledge relevant to the litigation.

---

## Core Principles

**These people are not your client.** Every letter must reflect that. You cannot give them legal advice, you cannot create an impression that you represent their interests, and you cannot do anything that looks like coaching or inducement. Your goal is cooperation, and cooperation flows from clarity, respect, and minimal imposition.

**Tone baseline:** Professional, courteous, plain-language. Assume the recipient has never been involved in litigation before. Avoid legalese unless you are quoting a document they have received (e.g., a subpoena), and when you do, explain it immediately.

---

## First Contact Letters

The first letter sets the relationship. Get these elements right:

1. **Who you are.** Name, firm, role. One sentence.
2. **Why you are writing.** Identify the lawsuit at a high level -- parties, general subject matter. Only enough detail for them to understand why they might have relevant information.
3. **Why them specifically.** State plainly what connection they have to the facts. ("You were present at the intersection of Main and 4th on June 12, 2024" or "You supervised the plaintiff during her employment at XYZ Corp.")
4. **What you are asking for.** Be specific. A phone call? A meeting? Written answers? Documents? Do not leave them guessing.
5. **What happens next.** Tell them you will follow up by phone in a few days, or ask them to call you. Give a specific timeframe.
6. **Reassurance.** Make clear this is voluntary at this stage (if it is). Let them know they are free to consult their own lawyer if they wish.

**Do not** include a detailed summary of your client's position. Do not characterize the evidence. Do not tell them what you expect them to say.

---

## Subpoena / Summons Accompaniment Letters

When you serve a subpoena to testify or produce documents, always include a cover letter. The subpoena alone is intimidating and confusing to most people.

The letter should:

- **Acknowledge the formality.** ("Enclosed is a Subpoena to Witness issued by the Supreme Court of British Columbia. I understand this may be unfamiliar, and I want to explain what it means in practical terms.")
- **Explain the obligation in plain language.** They are legally required to attend or produce documents. This is a standard part of the litigation process. It does not mean they are in trouble.
- **State the date, time, and location clearly.** Repeat this even though it is on the subpoena. Bold it or set it apart.
- **Explain conduct money / witness fees.** If you are enclosing conduct money, say so and explain what it is for.
- **Offer to discuss scheduling.** If there is any flexibility, say so. If there is not, be honest about that too.
- **Invite them to call with questions.** Give a direct phone number.
- **Note their right to seek independent legal advice.** One sentence is enough.

**Do not** minimize the legal obligation. Do not say "this is just a formality" if noncompliance has consequences. Be honest but not threatening.

---

## Scheduling Interviews or Examinations for Discovery

When arranging an interview (informal) or examination for discovery (formal, under the Rules):

- **Distinguish between the two.** If you are asking for a voluntary interview, say so. If you are scheduling an examination for discovery under the Rules, explain what that entails -- they will be under oath, there will be a court reporter, opposing counsel will be present.
- **Propose multiple dates and times.** Show you are willing to accommodate their schedule.
- **Estimate duration.** Give your honest best estimate. Do not say "15 minutes" if you know it will be two hours.
- **Explain logistics.** Where, how to get there, parking, whether it can be done by video, whether they need to bring anything.
- **For formal examinations**, enclose or reference the relevant notice or order and explain what it means.

---

## Thank-You Letters

Send one after every interview, examination, or trial appearance. These are short and genuine.

- Thank them for their time.
- Acknowledge that participating in litigation is inconvenient.
- If there may be further involvement, say so honestly. ("You may be asked to attend the trial, which is currently scheduled for [date]. I will be in touch well in advance if that becomes necessary.")
- If their involvement is complete, tell them.

This is not just courtesy -- it preserves goodwill for future contact if needed.

---

## Reluctant or Uncooperative Witnesses

Tone shift: firmer, but still professional. Never hostile.

**Escalation sequence:**

1. **Second request (letter or call).** Reiterate the ask. Acknowledge they may be busy. Emphasize the importance of their evidence to a fair resolution.
2. **Formal notice.** If voluntary cooperation fails, let them know you may need to compel their attendance or production through a subpoena. Frame this as a last resort, not a threat. ("I would prefer to arrange this at a time convenient to you rather than through a formal court process.")
3. **Subpoena.** If you reach this stage, the cover letter should be straightforward: you attempted voluntary cooperation, it was not possible, and you are now compelled to use this process. Remain respectful.

**Never:**
- Threaten consequences beyond what the law actually provides.
- Imply they will be held in contempt simply for being uncooperative before a subpoena is issued.
- Suggest their reluctance will be used against them or disclosed to the court in a way that damages them.

---

## What NOT to Do

This is non-negotiable:

- **Do not coach.** Never suggest what the witness should say, emphasize, or avoid. Do not send them documents "to refresh their memory" in a way that shapes their testimony.
- **Do not offer inducements.** No gifts, no favours, no promises beyond what the Rules require (conduct money, witness fees). Even the appearance of inducement is a problem.
- **Do not give legal advice.** You are not their lawyer. Do not advise them on their rights, obligations, or potential exposure. If they ask, tell them to consult their own counsel.
- **Do not create a solicitor-client impression.** Avoid language like "I'm here to help you" or "let me take care of this for you." Be clear about who you act for.
- **Do not discuss other witnesses' evidence.** Do not tell Witness A what Witness B said.
- **Do not pressure.** Persistent follow-up is fine. Harassment is not. Know the line.

---

## Ethical Guardrails

- Under the BC Code of Professional Conduct (Chapter 7), you must not deceive or mislead a witness, and you must not discourage a witness from giving evidence or cooperating with the other side.
- An independent witness is entitled to speak with either side. You cannot ask them to speak only with you.
- If a witness tells you they have retained counsel, all further communication goes through that counsel.
- Document your interactions. If a conversation is important, confirm it in writing afterward.

## Examples

### Example 1: First Contact Letter

> Dear Ms. Chen,
>
> I am a lawyer at Logica Law and I represent [Client Name] in a civil claim arising from a motor vehicle collision that occurred on March 15, 2025, at the intersection of Granville Street and West Broadway in Vancouver.
>
> I am writing to you because I understand you may have witnessed the collision. Your account of what happened would be very helpful in resolving this matter fairly.
>
> I would appreciate the opportunity to speak with you briefly by telephone at a time that is convenient for you. The call would likely take no more than 15 to 20 minutes. You are under no obligation to speak with me, and you are welcome to consult with a lawyer of your own before deciding.
>
> I will try to reach you by phone next week. In the meantime, please feel free to contact me directly at [phone] or [email] if you have any questions.
>
> Thank you for your time.

### Example 2: Subpoena Cover Letter

> Dear Mr. Okafor,
>
> Enclosed please find a Subpoena to Witness issued out of the Supreme Court of British Columbia in the matter of [Style of Cause], Court File No. [X].
>
> I understand that receiving a subpoena can be unexpected. In practical terms, it means you are being asked to attend and give evidence at the trial of this matter. This is a routine part of the litigation process and does not mean you have done anything wrong or that you are in any legal difficulty.
>
> **You are required to attend at the Vancouver Law Courts, 800 Smithe Street, on May 5, 2026, at 10:00 a.m.** Please plan to be available for the full day, although your testimony may take less time.
>
> Enclosed is conduct money in the amount of $[X] to cover your travel and daily attendance, as required by the Rules of Court.
>
> If the scheduled date poses a significant hardship, please contact me as soon as possible and I will do my best to accommodate your schedule. You are also entitled to seek independent legal advice about this subpoena if you wish.
>
> Please do not hesitate to call me at [phone] with any questions.

### Example 3: Follow-Up to a Reluctant Witness

> Dear Ms. Patel,
>
> I wrote to you on February 10 and followed up by telephone on February 18 regarding the above matter. I have not yet heard back from you and I understand you may be busy.
>
> I am reaching out again because your evidence is important to the fair resolution of this case. I would be happy to arrange a brief call at whatever time works best for you, including evenings or weekends if that is more convenient.
>
> If I am unable to arrange a voluntary interview, I may need to have a subpoena issued to secure your attendance. I would much prefer to avoid that step and work with your schedule directly.
>
> Please contact me at your earliest convenience at [phone] or [email].

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Before sending any letter to an independent witness, confirm:

- [ ] You have identified yourself and your client clearly
- [ ] You have stated the purpose of the letter in plain language
- [ ] You have not characterized the evidence or suggested what they should say
- [ ] You have not offered anything beyond what the Rules require
- [ ] You have reminded them of their right to independent legal advice
- [ ] You have provided clear contact information and next steps
- [ ] The tone is appropriate to the stage of engagement (warm on first contact, firmer if follow-up is needed, always professional)
