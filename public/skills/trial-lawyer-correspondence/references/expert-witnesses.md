# Correspondence with Expert Witnesses

## Audience Profile

Expert witnesses are professionals retained to provide opinion evidence in litigation. They include forensic accountants, engineers, medical specialists, valuators, actuaries, and other specialists. They are highly credentialed professionals and must be treated as peers, not subordinates.

**Core principle:** Every piece of written communication with an expert is potentially producible. Write accordingly.

---

## Tone and Register

- **Collegial and professional.** Use "Dr. [Name]" or the expert's professional title until they invite first-name use.
- Respect their expertise. Frame requests as questions, not instructions. Say "I would appreciate your opinion on..." rather than "Please conclude that..."
- Be precise and organized. Experts bill for their time. Do not waste it with vague requests or disorganized materials.
- Avoid legalese when explaining the factual context. Translate legal concepts into plain language where needed.

---

## Types of Correspondence

### 1. Initial Retainer Letter

This is the foundational document. It sets the terms and scope of the engagement. Include:

- **Identity of the retaining party and the case.** Name the client, the action, the court file number if one exists.
- **Scope of work.** Define the specific question(s) you are asking the expert to address. Be precise. A vague scope leads to an unfocused report.
- **The expert's duty to the court.** Under BC Supreme Court Civil Rule 11-2, the expert's primary duty is to the court, not to the retaining party. State this explicitly. The expert must certify this duty in any report they produce.
- **Materials to be provided.** List what you will send, or describe what categories of documents the expert should expect to receive.
- **Timeline.** State the deadline for the report and any interim milestones (e.g., draft report, meeting to discuss preliminary findings).
- **Fees and billing.** Confirm the expert's hourly rate, retainer amount (if any), billing frequency, and any caps or approvals required before exceeding a threshold.
- **Confidentiality.** The materials are provided in confidence for the purpose of the litigation. The expert should not disclose them to third parties without your authorization.
- **Disclosure obligations.** Advise the expert that their report and, in most circumstances, the instructions provided to them will be producible to the other side. This is critical context for how they conduct their work and what they put in writing.

### 2. Covering Letters for Document Production to Experts

When sending materials to the expert:

- Number or index the documents. Provide a clear list of what is enclosed.
- Identify new materials vs. previously provided materials.
- Flag specific documents that are most relevant to the expert's mandate, but do not characterize what those documents prove.
- If documents contain privileged material that has been inadvertently included, flag this and provide instructions.

### 3. Instructions for a Specific Question

When adding a new question or redirecting the expert's focus:

- State the question clearly and neutrally.
- Provide any additional factual assumptions the expert should make (and label them as assumptions).
- Do not embed your theory of the case in the question. Ask "What is the fair market value of X as of [date]?" not "Please confirm that the value of X is approximately $Y."
- If the question arises from something the opposing expert has said, you may provide the opposing report and ask for a response, but do not tell your expert what their answer should be.

### 4. Follow-Up on Draft Reports

When reviewing a draft report and requesting revisions:

- Ask questions. Do not direct conclusions.
- You may point out factual errors (e.g., "The date of the accident was March 3, not March 13 -- does this affect your analysis?").
- You may ask the expert to clarify ambiguous passages or to address points they have not covered.
- You may ask whether the expert has considered a particular fact or document.
- **Do not** ask the expert to change, strengthen, or soften a conclusion. If the conclusion is unhelpful, your options are to not tender the report or to ask whether additional information might affect the opinion. But the decision about what the opinion is belongs to the expert.
- Keep follow-up correspondence brief and specific. Numbered questions work well.

### 5. Preparing for Cross-Examination

- Oral preparation sessions are generally appropriate and protected by litigation privilege.
- **Do not put cross-examination preparation into writing.** A letter saying "opposing counsel will likely challenge you on X, and here is how you should respond" is producible and damaging.
- What you can put in writing: logistics (date, time, location of the preparation session), a list of the topics you intend to cover (framed neutrally), and copies of documents the expert should review in advance.
- Any substantive discussion of how the expert should handle particular challenges belongs in an in-person or telephone meeting, not in correspondence.

---

## What to Avoid

1. **Do not suggest conclusions.** Never write "we believe the value is $X" or "it would be helpful if your report concluded that..." This undermines the expert's independence and, if disclosed, destroys credibility.

2. **Do not share privileged litigation strategy.** The expert does not need to know your theory of the case, your assessment of witnesses, or your settlement position. If any of this ends up in the expert's file, it may be producible.

3. **Do not use the expert as a sounding board for legal arguments.** The expert opines on facts within their expertise. Legal conclusions are yours.

4. **Be careful about multiple drafts.** In BC, draft expert reports are generally producible if they reveal the substance of communications between counsel and the expert. Limit the number of drafts. Frame your feedback as questions, not as tracked-changes edits to the expert's own words.

5. **Do not cc the client on expert correspondence** unless there is a specific reason. It complicates privilege and disclosure issues.

---

## BC-Specific Requirements: Rule 11-2

Under the BC Supreme Court Civil Rules, Rule 11-2:

- An expert's report must include a signed certification that the expert is aware of their duty to assist the court and is not an advocate for any party.
- The expert must disclose the instructions provided to them (the substance of the questions asked and the facts and assumptions they were given).
- The report must include the expert's qualifications, the facts and assumptions relied upon, their opinion, and the reasons for it.

**Practical implication:** Every letter of instruction you write to an expert should be written with the assumption that opposing counsel and the court will read it. If you would not want to read it aloud in a courtroom, do not send it.

## Examples

### Example 1: Initial Retainer Letter

> Dear Dr. Okafor,
>
> Re: *Smith v. Jones*, Vancouver Registry No. S-224567
>
> I am writing to retain you as an expert in forensic accounting in the above action. I act for the plaintiff, Linda Smith.
>
> **Background:** This is a family law proceeding in which the division of business assets is in dispute. The respondent, David Jones, is the sole shareholder and director of Jones Construction Ltd. The central issue for your engagement is the fair market value of the company as of the date of separation, June 15, 2024.
>
> **Scope of work:** I am asking you to:
>
> 1. Review the financial records of Jones Construction Ltd. for the fiscal years 2020 through 2024.
> 2. Provide your opinion on the fair market value of the company as of June 15, 2024.
> 3. Identify and comment on any normalized adjustments you consider appropriate.
>
> **Materials:** I will provide the company's financial statements, corporate tax returns, and bank records under separate cover. If you require additional documents, please let me know.
>
> **Your duty to the court:** As you are aware, under Rule 11-2 of the BC Supreme Court Civil Rules, your duty as an expert is to assist the court. Your report must include a certification of this duty. Your opinion must be independent and based on your professional expertise, regardless of which party has retained you.
>
> **Disclosure:** Please be aware that your report and the substance of the instructions I provide to you will be disclosed to the opposing party. I ask that you keep this in mind in all of our communications.
>
> **Fees:** I understand your rate is $450 per hour. Please provide an estimate of the total fees for this engagement. I would appreciate advance notice if fees are likely to exceed $15,000.
>
> **Timeline:** The trial is scheduled for November 2026. I would appreciate receiving a draft report by June 30, 2026, so that we have adequate time to review it and address any questions before the report-delivery deadline.
>
> **Confidentiality:** All materials provided to you are confidential and should not be disclosed to any third party without my prior authorization.
>
> I look forward to working with you. Please confirm your availability and willingness to accept this retainer at your earliest convenience.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> Brahm Dorst
> Logica Law

### Example 2: Follow-Up on a Draft Report

> Dear Dr. Okafor,
>
> Re: *Smith v. Jones* -- Draft Valuation Report
>
> Thank you for providing your draft report dated May 15, 2026. I have reviewed it and have the following questions:
>
> 1. At page 12, you note that management salaries were "within a reasonable range." Are you able to specify the range you consider reasonable and explain your basis for that conclusion? This will help the court assess this aspect of your analysis.
>
> 2. I note the report does not address the shareholder loan of $85,000 shown on the 2023 balance sheet. Is this relevant to your valuation, and if so, how have you treated it?
>
> 3. At page 8, the date of the company's fiscal year-end is noted as March 31; however, the financial statements I provided indicate a December 31 year-end. Could you confirm which is correct and whether this affects your calculations?
>
> 4. Opposing counsel has disclosed a report from their expert, Mark Travers, CPA, which I enclose. You are not obligated to respond to it, but if there are aspects of Mr. Travers' methodology or conclusions that you believe warrant comment, I would welcome your observations.
>
> Please let me know if you have any questions or if you require additional materials.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> Brahm Dorst
> Logica Law

### Example 3: Instructions for a New Question

> Dear Dr. Okafor,
>
> Re: *Smith v. Jones* -- Additional Question
>
> A new issue has arisen in this proceeding that I would like your assistance with.
>
> The respondent has disclosed that Jones Construction Ltd. entered into a contract on May 1, 2024 -- approximately six weeks before the separation date -- to purchase a commercial property at 400 Industrial Road, Surrey, BC for $1,200,000. The purchase completed on August 15, 2024. I enclose a copy of the purchase agreement and the completion documents.
>
> I would appreciate your opinion on the following:
>
> 1. Should this property be included in your valuation of Jones Construction Ltd. as of the separation date (June 15, 2024)?
> 2. If so, at what value should it be included, given that the purchase had been agreed to but had not yet completed as of that date?
>
> For the purpose of this question, please assume that the separation date is not in dispute.
>
> If this additional question materially changes your fee estimate, please let me know before proceeding.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> Brahm Dorst
> Logica Law

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Before sending any correspondence to an expert, confirm:

- [ ] The letter does not suggest, imply, or direct any conclusion.
- [ ] The letter does not contain privileged litigation strategy or counsel's assessment of the case.
- [ ] The question(s) are stated neutrally and clearly.
- [ ] Factual assumptions are labelled as assumptions.
- [ ] You would be comfortable with opposing counsel and the court reading this letter.
- [ ] The letter references the expert's duty to the court (in initial retainer letters).
- [ ] Documents enclosed are listed and indexed.
