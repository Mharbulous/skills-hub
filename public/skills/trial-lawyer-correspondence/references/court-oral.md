# Court Oral Communication

Reference for in-court oral advocacy in BC Supreme Court, BC Court of Appeal, and BC Provincial Court. Covers how to address the court, structure submissions, handle questions from the bench, and maintain effective courtroom presence.

---

## Forms of Address

| Court | Address |
|---|---|
| BC Supreme Court | "My Lord" / "My Lady" |
| BC Court of Appeal | "My Lord" / "My Lady" (to individual justice); "My Lords," "My Ladies," or "My Lords and My Ladies" (to the panel) |
| BC Provincial Court | "Your Honour" |

Never say "Judge" or "Judge Smith" when addressing the court. When referring to a judge of another court in submissions, use "Justice Smith" or "Judge Smith" in the third person, but always use the proper form of address when speaking directly to the bench.

---

## Opening a Hearing

### Introduce Yourself and Your Client

Stand. State your name, your firm, and who you represent. Be brief. The court does not need your life story.

> "My Lady, Brahm Dorst, appearing for the plaintiff, [Client Name]."

If opposing counsel has not yet appeared, you may note that. If there are multiple parties, wait for the registrar to call the matter or for the judge to invite introductions in order.

### State the Nature of the Application

After introductions, tell the judge what you are asking for in one or two sentences. Frame it so the judge immediately understands the issue and the relief sought.

> "My Lady, this is an application by the plaintiff for an order compelling production of documents identified in categories 3 through 7 of the plaintiff's list of documents. The application is brought under Rule 7-1(1)."

Do not launch into argument. Let the judge orient to the matter first.

## Structuring Your Argument

### Lead with Your Strongest Point

Judges form impressions quickly. Put your best argument first. If you bury it at point four of five, you may never get there.

### Use a Roadmap

Tell the judge what you intend to cover and roughly how long you expect to take. This gives the judge a framework for listening.

> "My Lord, I intend to address three points: first, the applicable legal test; second, why the evidence satisfies that test; and third, why the balance of convenience favours granting the injunction. I expect to be approximately 15 minutes."

### Be Concrete

Refer to specific paragraphs of affidavits, specific tabs in your brief, and specific passages of authorities. Do not make the judge hunt for the evidence that supports your point.

> "If My Lady would turn to Tab 4 of the application record, paragraph 12 of the affidavit of Ms. Chen, she deposes that..."

### Know When to Stop

If you have made your point, stop. Repeating yourself does not make the argument stronger. It signals that you are not confident the judge understood you, which is either insulting or an admission that your point is weak.

## Responding to Questions from the Bench

This is the single most important skill in oral advocacy.

### Rule 1: Stop Talking Immediately

When a judge begins to speak, stop mid-sentence if necessary. Do not finish your thought. Do not hold up a finger. Stop.

### Rule 2: Answer the Question

Answer the judge's actual question. Do not pivot to a point you would rather make. If the question is "What is your authority for that proposition?", the answer is a case name or a statutory provision, not a restatement of your argument.

If you do not know the answer, say so directly:

> "My Lord, I do not have the answer to that at hand. I am prepared to provide it in written submissions if the court would find that helpful."

### Rule 3: Then Return to Your Argument

Once you have answered, you may return to your prepared submissions. A brief transition works:

> "Returning to the second branch of the test, My Lady..."

### What Questions Really Mean

- A question about your weakest point means the judge has spotted it. Address it honestly. Do not dodge.
- A question that restates your argument means the judge may be onside and is testing whether they have understood correctly. Confirm and build on it.
- Repeated questions on the same point mean the judge is not satisfied with your answer. Try a different angle. Do not simply repeat yourself louder.

## Reading the Room

### When the Judge Is Skeptical

You will know. The questions will be pointed. The tone may shift. Do not become defensive. Acknowledge the difficulty and explain why your position holds despite it.

> "My Lady, I accept that the timeline is unusual. However, the evidence at paragraph 14 of the affidavit explains the delay, and the prejudice to the defendant from permitting the amendment is minimal for the reasons I have outlined."

### When the Judge Has Made Up Their Mind Against You

Sometimes the decision is effectively made before you finish. You will sense it. At that point, your job shifts from persuading to preserving the record for appeal.

- State your key legal propositions clearly and concisely.
- Identify the specific findings of fact or law you submit the court should make.
- Ask that your written argument be marked as part of the record if it has not already been filed.
- Do not argue with the judge or express frustration.

### When the Judge Is With You

Do not oversell. Thank the court and sit down. Excess argument when you are winning risks giving the judge a reason to reconsider.

---

## Conceding Gracefully

Conceding a weak point builds credibility on your strong ones. Judges notice when counsel cannot bring themselves to concede anything.

> "My Lord, I accept that the second factor does not favour my client. However, I submit that the first and third factors are determinative, and both weigh strongly in favour of the relief sought."

Never concede a point that is fatal to your case. But if a point is merely unfavourable and you have stronger ground elsewhere, acknowledge it and move on.

---

## Tone

### What to Aim For

Respectful but confident. You are an officer of the court assisting the judge in resolving a dispute. You are not begging, performing, or fighting.

### What to Avoid

- **Sycophantic**: "If it please Your Lordship, and with the greatest of respect, I would most humbly submit..." -- This wastes time and sounds obsequious.
- **Combative**: Arguing with the judge, sighing audibly, eye-rolling, or using a sarcastic tone. This never works. It damages your client's case and your reputation.
- **Overly casual**: The courtroom is formal. Do not use slang, do not joke unless the judge initiates humour, and do not address opposing counsel by first name in front of the bench.

### The Right Register

Formal but natural. Speak in complete sentences. Avoid legalese where plain language is clearer. "I submit" is fine. "It is respectfully submitted that the learned chambers judge erred in principle" is fine. "With the greatest respect, we would humbly venture to suggest" is too much.

## Courtroom Etiquette

- **Stand** when addressing the court.
- **Sit** when the judge is speaking to you or to opposing counsel, unless directed otherwise.
- **Wait your turn.** Do not interrupt opposing counsel's submissions. Take notes and address their points in reply.
- **Bow** when entering and leaving the courtroom (a slight nod toward the bench if the judge is seated; toward the coat of arms if the bench is empty).
- **Do not eat, drink, or use your phone** while court is in session. Water at counsel table is generally acceptable.
- **Rise** when the judge enters or leaves the courtroom.

---

## Handling Unexpected Rulings or Interruptions

If the judge rules against you on a point mid-hearing:

1. Stay composed. Do not react visibly.
2. Take a note of the ruling.
3. If the ruling affects the rest of your argument, ask for a brief moment to reorganize.
4. If you need to preserve the point for appeal, state clearly: "My Lord, for the record, the plaintiff's position is [X]. I understand the court's ruling and will proceed on that basis."

If the judge asks you to move on from a topic, move on. You have made your point. Pressing further after a clear signal will only irritate the court.

## Calibrating Length

### Short Submissions (5-10 minutes)

Appropriate for routine chambers applications: adjournments, consent orders, straightforward procedural matters, case planning conferences.

Get to the point. State what you want, why you are entitled to it, and sit down.

### Full Oral Argument (15-45 minutes)

Appropriate for contested interlocutory applications, summary judgment or summary trial, and appellate hearings. Structure matters here. Use the roadmap approach described above.

Watch the bench. If the judge is nodding and not asking questions, they may already agree. Shorten your submissions. If they are asking many questions, you may need more time -- ask for it politely.

> "My Lady, I had estimated 15 minutes, but given the court's questions I may require an additional 10 minutes. Would that be acceptable?"

### Reply Submissions

Reply is for responding to new points raised by the other side, not for repeating your original argument. Keep it brief. If opposing counsel raised nothing new, say so:

> "My Lord, I have nothing in reply."

That is a powerful statement. It signals confidence.

## Illustrative Examples

### Example 1: Opening a Chambers Application

> "My Lady, Brahm Dorst for the plaintiff, ABC Corp. My friend Mr. Patel appears for the defendant.
>
> This is the plaintiff's application for an interim injunction restraining the defendant from disposing of assets pending trial. The application is supported by the affidavit of Ms. Rivera, sworn March 12, 2026, found at Tab 2 of the application record.
>
> I intend to address three points: the legal test for an interim injunction under the RJR-MacDonald framework, the evidence establishing a serious question to be tried, and the balance of convenience. I expect to be approximately 20 minutes.
>
> Turning to the first point, the test is well established..."

### Example 2: Responding to a Difficult Question from the Bench

The judge interrupts mid-submission:

> **Court**: "Mr. Dorst, your client waited eight months before bringing this application. How is that consistent with the urgency you say supports an injunction?"
>
> **Counsel**: "My Lord, the delay is addressed at paragraphs 8 through 11 of Ms. Rivera's affidavit. The plaintiff became aware of the asset transfers only in January 2026 and retained counsel within two weeks. The application was filed 30 days later. In my submission, the timeline from discovery to application is consistent with urgency. The eight-month period your Lordship refers to predates the plaintiff's knowledge.
>
> Returning to the balance of convenience..."

Key elements: the question is answered directly, with a specific evidentiary reference, and counsel does not become defensive about the delay. The transition back to the prepared argument is smooth.

### Example 3: Brief Reply Submission

Opposing counsel has argued that the plaintiff delayed and that the balance of convenience favours the defendant because the injunction would disrupt their business operations.

> "My Lady, briefly in reply on two points.
>
> First, on delay, my friend relies on the date of the underlying transaction, but the relevant date is when my client discovered the asset transfers. That evidence is uncontradicted at paragraph 9 of Ms. Rivera's affidavit.
>
> Second, on the balance of convenience, my friend says the injunction would disrupt the defendant's operations, but he has filed no evidence of that disruption. There is no affidavit from the defendant. The court is being asked to accept bare assertions from the bar.
>
> Those are my submissions."

Key elements: reply addresses only what opposing counsel raised, is concise, and ends with a clear signal that counsel is finished.

---

## Summary Principles

1. Be prepared. Know your facts, your law, and your record.
2. Be brief. Say what you need to say and stop.
3. Answer questions directly. The judge's question is more important than your outline.
4. Concede what you should. Fight on the ground that matters.
5. Stay composed. The courtroom rewards discipline, not drama.
6. Respect the process. You are an officer of the court first.
