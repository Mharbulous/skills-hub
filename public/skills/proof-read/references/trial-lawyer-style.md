# Trial Lawyer Communication Style Guide

## Core Principles

1. **Appropriate tone for audience** - Adapt formality and approach based on recipient
2. **Clear boundaries** - Be diplomatic but firm; never weaken established positions
3. **Professional consistency** - Maintain respect and civility across all communications
4. **Precision over promises** - Avoid overcommitting or creating unrealistic expectations
5. **Balanced advocacy** - Be fair-minded while remaining firm in representing client interests

## Audience-Specific Guidelines

### Communications to Clients

**Tone:** Professional, accessible, reassuring but realistic

**Key considerations:**
- Set clear expectations about process, timelines, and outcomes
- Establish boundaries around communication frequency and scope
- Avoid legal jargon unless necessary; explain when used
- Be direct about limitations and risks
- Never overpromise results or guarantees

**Boundary-setting language:**
- "I appreciate your concerns. To best serve you, I need to..."
- "Our agreement provides that..."
- "To manage this effectively, here's what I need from you..."
- "I understand this is frustrating. Here's what we can realistically expect..."

### Communications to Opposing Counsel

**Tone:** Professional, courteous, firm

**Key considerations:**
- Maintain collegiality while advocating zealously
- Be clear about positions without being combative
- Document agreements and disagreements precisely
- Set deadlines and expectations clearly
- Stand firm on boundaries without escalating unnecessarily

**Effective language:**
- "I trust we can resolve this professionally..."
- "My client's position remains..."
- "Unless I hear from you by [date], I will proceed with..."
- "While I appreciate your perspective, we must respectfully disagree..."
- "To avoid misunderstanding, let me clarify our position..."

### Communications to Witnesses

**Tone:** Professional, neutral, clear

**Key considerations:**
- Avoid leading or appearing to coach
- Maintain objectivity and professionalism
- Provide clear instructions about process
- Document cooperation or lack thereof
- Remain courteous regardless of witness's attitude

### Communications to the Court

**Tone:** Formal, respectful, precise

**Key considerations:**
- Utmost professionalism and deference
- Precise language with proper citations
- Avoid hyperbole or emotional language
- Be concise and organized
- Never misrepresent facts or law

## Common Style Issues to Address

### Passive Voice
- Generally avoid passive constructions
- Exception: When actor is unknown or when diplomacy requires it
- Replace "mistakes were made" with "we made mistakes" (when taking responsibility)
- Replace "your client was seen" with "witnesses observed your client"

### Hedging Language
**Eliminate weak qualifiers when stating positions:**
- "I think" → state directly
- "It seems to me" → state directly
- "Perhaps we could" → "We should" or "We propose"
- "I'm just wondering if" → "Please clarify whether"

**Preserve appropriate hedging when discussing uncertain outcomes:**
- Keep "may," "likely," "possible" when discussing predictions
- Keep "appears," "suggests" when discussing evidence

### Over-Apologizing
- Remove unnecessary apologies ("Sorry to bother you")
- Keep apologies when genuinely needed for errors or delays
- Replace "I'm sorry but" with direct statement

### Boundary Weakening
**Watch for phrases that undermine positions:**
- "I hate to bring this up, but..." → State directly
- "If it's not too much trouble..." → Make clear request
- "When you get a chance..." → Provide specific deadline
- "I don't want to seem difficult..." → Remove and state position

**Maintain these important qualifications:**
- Good-faith disagreements on legal points
- Acknowledgment of legitimate constraints
- Professional courtesies that don't compromise position

## Diplomacy Without Weakness

**Effective diplomatic language:**
- "I understand your position. However, we maintain that..."
- "While I appreciate the challenge this presents, our position remains..."
- "To resolve this efficiently, we propose..."
- "In the interest of judicial economy..."

**Avoid:**
- Aggressive or threatening language
- Personal attacks
- Emotional appeals in professional correspondence
- Ultimatums unless absolutely necessary and carefully considered

## Red Flags to Address

1. **Unclear commitments** - Every commitment should be specific
2. **Missing deadlines** - Every request should include a timeframe
3. **Ambiguous positions** - Legal positions should be clear and documented
4. **Apologetic tone for legitimate positions** - Remove unnecessary apologies
5. **Inconsistent boundaries** - Ensure positions align with prior communications
