# Output Templates for Judicial Decision Analysis

This document provides formatting guidance and examples for each analysis task.

## Task 1: Facts as Found

**Format:** Clear narrative summarizing the factual findings made by the judge or trier of fact.

**Structure:**
- Lead with the core factual scenario
- Present facts chronologically when possible
- Distinguish between background facts and disputed facts that were determined
- Note any credibility findings or evidentiary rulings that influenced fact-finding

**Example:**
> The trial judge found that on March 15, 2023, the defendant drove through a red light at the intersection of Main Street and 5th Avenue at approximately 45 mph in a 30 mph zone. The judge accepted the testimony of two eyewitnesses who observed the defendant's vehicle accelerate through the intersection after the light had turned red. The judge found the defendant's testimony that the light was yellow to lack credibility, noting inconsistencies with the physical evidence regarding brake marks.

## Task 2: Alternative Facts Rejected

**Format:** Summary of factual contentions that were disputed but ultimately rejected by the court.

**Structure:**
- Identify which party asserted the alternative facts
- State the rejected factual contention clearly
- Note the reason for rejection (credibility, lack of evidence, contradicted by other evidence)
- Distinguish between facts rejected on credibility vs. legal grounds

**Example:**
> The court rejected the defendant's contention that the traffic light was yellow when he entered the intersection. This version of events was contradicted by two independent eyewitnesses and was inconsistent with the defendant's own prior statement to police at the scene. The court also rejected the defendant's claim that he was driving at the speed limit, finding the physical evidence of brake marks indicated a higher speed.

## Task 3: Legal Principles Applied to Find Facts

**Format:** Summary of evidentiary rules, burdens of proof, and legal standards that governed the fact-finding process.

**Structure:**
- Identify the applicable burden of proof and standard
- Note any evidentiary principles or rules that were applied
- Explain how these principles influenced the fact-finding
- Include presumptions or inferences if relevant

**Example:**
> The court applied the civil standard of proof on a balance of probabilities. In assessing witness credibility, the court applied the principles from *Faryna v. Chorny*, considering the consistency of testimony with surrounding circumstances and the witness's demeanor. The court also applied the principle that where a party has access to evidence but fails to produce it, an adverse inference may be drawn.

## Task 4: Legal Principles Applied by the Judge

**Format:** Summary of the substantive legal rules, tests, and doctrines applied to resolve the legal issues.

**Structure:**
- Identify the legal issue(s) addressed
- State the applicable legal test or standard
- Explain how the court applied the law to the facts
- Include the source of the legal principle (case law, statute)
- Present in logical order following the court's reasoning

**Example:**
> The court applied the test for negligence established in *Donoghue v. Stevenson*: (1) duty of care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) causation, and (4) damages. The court found that drivers owe a duty of care to other road users, and that driving through a red light at excessive speed breaches the standard of care expected of a reasonable driver. Applying the "but for" test for causation from *Clements v. Clements*, the court found that but for the defendant's breach, the accident would not have occurred.

## Task 5: Legal Principles Rejected or Distinguished

**Format:** Summary of legal arguments, precedents, or principles that were considered but not applied.

**Structure:**
- Identify which party advanced the rejected principle
- State the legal argument or precedent clearly
- Explain why the court rejected or distinguished it
- Note whether it was distinguished on facts or rejected on legal grounds

**Example:**
> The defendant relied on *Smith v. Jones*, arguing that contributory negligence should reduce damages where a plaintiff fails to wear a seatbelt. The court distinguished *Smith*, noting that case involved a rear-end collision where seatbelt use would have prevented injury, whereas in the present case, the medical evidence established that a seatbelt would not have mitigated the specific injuries sustained. The court rejected the defendant's alternative argument that the doctrine of inevitable accident applied, finding that the doctrine requires circumstances beyond the defendant's control, which was not established here.

## Task 7: Order Pronounced

**Format:** Clear statement of the court's disposition and any ancillary orders.

**Structure:**
- State the main order (judgment for plaintiff/defendant, damages awarded, etc.)
- Include any injunctive relief or specific performance ordered
- Detail costs awards with specificity (amount, scale, timing)
- Note any stays, implementation timelines, or conditions
- Include any directions for further proceedings

**Example:**
> The court ordered judgment for the plaintiff in the amount of $75,000 in damages. Costs were awarded to the plaintiff on Scale B, payable within 30 days. The defendant was ordered to pay pre-judgment interest at the rate of 3% per annum from the date of the accident (March 15, 2023) to the date of judgment. The court declined to grant a stay of execution pending appeal.

## General Formatting Guidelines

- Use clear, professional legal writing
- Maintain objectivity - report what the court found/held
- Use precise legal terminology
- Include pinpoint citations where available
- Break complex issues into digestible paragraphs
- Use headings to organize multi-issue decisions
