# Correspondence to Opposing Parties

Reference guidance for drafting correspondence directly to the person or entity on the other side of a dispute — not their lawyer. This is one of the most ethically sensitive categories of legal correspondence.

---

## When You Can and Cannot Write Directly

### The Core Rule: Represented Parties Are Off-Limits

Under the BC Code of Professional Conduct (Rule 7.2-6), you **must not** communicate directly with a person you know is represented by a lawyer on the matter, except:

1. **With prior consent of their lawyer.** Get this in writing.
2. **On matters outside the scope of the representation.** Narrow exception — be cautious. If the opposing party's lawyer was retained only for the contract dispute, you may be able to contact the party directly about an unrelated collections matter. But if there is any overlap, go through counsel.
3. **The representation has ended.** Confirm this before writing. A simple "I understand you are no longer represented by [lawyer] in this matter — please confirm" at the top of your letter is prudent.

If the party is **unrepresented**, you may write directly. But you must not:
- State or imply you are disinterested
- Give them legal advice
- Mislead them about their legal position

### Formerly Represented Parties

If the opposing party was previously represented but the retainer has ended:
- Confirm the lawyer is no longer on the record (check court filings if litigation is active)
- Your first letter should note: "We understand [former counsel] no longer represents you in this matter. If that is incorrect, please have your lawyer contact us immediately."
- Do not assume the party is now unsophisticated — they may retain new counsel at any time

---

## Tone and Approach

### What to Aim For

- **Formal and firm.** You represent your client's interests. Be direct about what is owed, what is required, and what happens next.
- **Authoritative, not aggressive.** State the legal position plainly. Let the facts and the law do the work — do not editorialize or moralize.
- **Professional restraint.** Never write anything you would not want a judge to read. Assume every letter will become an exhibit.

### What to Avoid

- **Threats of criminal prosecution to gain advantage in a civil matter.** This is a professional conduct violation (Rule 5.1-6). Do not write "pay or we will report you to the police."
- **Personal attacks or inflammatory language.** "Your conduct has been dishonest and reprehensible" is unnecessary. "Your failure to perform under the contract has caused our client significant loss" says the same thing without the editorializing.
- **Ultimatums with no legal basis.** "If you do not pay by Friday we will ensure you never work in this industry again" is both unenforceable and potentially actionable. Stick to consequences you can actually deliver: filing a claim, registering a lien, pursuing enforcement.
- **Legal advice to the opposing party.** Do not explain what their legal options are, what defences they might have, or what a court would likely do to them. You act for your client, not theirs.
- **Anything that could be construed as harassment.** Repeated letters with escalating threats, contact at unusual hours, or communication designed to embarrass rather than advance the claim.

## Demand Letters

### Structure

A demand letter to an opposing party should follow this order:

1. **Identification.** Who you are, who you act for, what the matter concerns.
2. **Factual background.** Brief, accurate summary of the relevant facts. Do not overstate your case — this letter may be an exhibit at trial.
3. **Legal basis.** The cause of action or legal right your client relies on. Keep it concise — you are putting them on notice, not writing a legal memo.
4. **The demand.** Specific, clear, and quantified where possible. "Our client demands payment of $47,250.00, representing the unpaid balance under the Agreement dated March 15, 2024."
5. **Deadline.** Reasonable in the circumstances. 14 days is standard for most civil demands. 7 days may be appropriate for urgent matters. Specify a calendar date, not just "14 days."
6. **Consequences of non-compliance.** State what your client intends to do if the demand is not met. "Failing receipt of payment by [date], our client will commence legal proceedings without further notice" is the standard formulation.
7. **Reservation of rights.** "Our client reserves all rights and remedies available at law and in equity."
8. **Direction to seek legal advice.** When writing to an unrepresented party, include: "We recommend you obtain independent legal advice regarding this matter."

### Key Principles

- Be specific about amounts, dates, and obligations.
- Reference the underlying agreement, invoice, or obligation by date and description.
- Attach copies of key documents where it strengthens the demand.
- Do not overstate damages or include speculative heads of loss — keep to what you can prove.

## Pre-Litigation Correspondence

Before a lawsuit is filed, your correspondence serves several purposes:

- **Crystallize the claim.** Put the opposing party on notice of the specific allegations and the relief sought.
- **Start the clock on any pre-action requirements.** Some claims require notice periods (e.g., builders lien, municipal claims, strata disputes).
- **Demonstrate reasonableness.** A judge will want to see that your client tried to resolve the matter before litigating.
- **Preserve limitation arguments.** A clear demand letter helps establish when the cause of action was known and acknowledged.

Pre-litigation letters should be calibrated: firm enough to be taken seriously, measured enough that a court would view your client as the reasonable party.

---

## Notices of Default

When the opposing party has a contractual obligation and has failed to perform:

- Reference the specific contractual provision breached
- Describe the default with particularity (dates, amounts, performance milestones missed)
- State the cure period if the contract provides one
- State the consequences under the contract if the default is not cured (termination, acceleration, forfeiture of deposit, etc.)
- Preserve your client's right to pursue all available remedies

## Examples

### Example 1: Demand Letter — Unpaid Invoices

> **WITHOUT PREJUDICE**
>
> Dear Mr. Chen,
>
> We are counsel to Pacific Ridge Construction Ltd. ("Pacific Ridge") regarding outstanding amounts owing by you under the residential renovation contract dated June 3, 2025 (the "Contract").
>
> Under the Contract, you agreed to pay for completed work within 30 days of invoicing. Pacific Ridge has issued the following invoices, which remain unpaid:
>
> | Invoice No. | Date | Amount |
> |---|---|---|
> | PR-2025-0412 | August 15, 2025 | $18,500.00 |
> | PR-2025-0489 | September 20, 2025 | $12,750.00 |
>
> The total outstanding balance is **$31,250.00**. Despite repeated requests for payment, no payment has been received.
>
> We hereby demand payment of $31,250.00 on or before **April 30, 2026**. Failing receipt of payment by that date, our client will commence legal proceedings against you in the Supreme Court of British Columbia without further notice to you. Our client will seek the outstanding amount together with pre-judgment interest under the *Court Order Interest Act*, costs, and any further relief the Court considers appropriate.
>
> Our client reserves all rights and remedies available at law and in equity, including the right to claim additional damages for delay.
>
> We recommend you obtain independent legal advice regarding this matter.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> **Brahm Dorst**
> Logica Law

### Example 2: Notice of Default Under Contract

> Dear Ms. Patel,
>
> We act for Greenway Holdings Ltd. ("Greenway") in connection with the Commercial Lease dated January 10, 2024 between Greenway, as landlord, and you, as tenant, for Unit 204 — 1550 Marine Drive, West Vancouver, BC (the "Lease").
>
> Please be advised that you are in default of the Lease in the following respects:
>
> 1. **Unpaid rent.** Rent for the months of February and March 2026, totalling $9,400.00, remains outstanding.
> 2. **Unauthorized alterations.** Greenway has become aware that you have made structural modifications to the premises without the landlord's prior written consent, contrary to section 12.1 of the Lease.
>
> Pursuant to section 18.2 of the Lease, you have **fifteen (15) days** from the date of this letter to cure the above defaults by (a) paying all outstanding rent, and (b) restoring the premises to their original condition or obtaining Greenway's written consent to the alterations.
>
> If the defaults are not cured within the cure period, Greenway will exercise its rights under section 19 of the Lease, including the right to terminate the Lease, re-enter the premises, and pursue all available remedies for damages.
>
> Our client reserves all rights and remedies under the Lease, at law, and in equity.
>
> We recommend you obtain independent legal advice regarding this matter.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> **Brahm Dorst**
> Logica Law

### Example 3: Pre-Litigation Warning — Breach of Non-Competition Agreement

> **WITHOUT PREJUDICE**
>
> Dear Mr. Okafor,
>
> We are counsel to Northshore Digital Solutions Inc. ("Northshore"). This letter concerns your obligations under the Employment Agreement dated April 1, 2023 between you and Northshore (the "Agreement"), specifically the non-competition and non-solicitation covenants at sections 11 and 12.
>
> Your employment with Northshore ended on January 15, 2026. It has come to Northshore's attention that you have since joined Apex Software Ltd., a direct competitor, and that you have contacted at least three of Northshore's current clients to solicit their business.
>
> This conduct is in direct breach of your contractual obligations. The non-competition covenant at section 11 of the Agreement restricts you from engaging in a competing business within the Lower Mainland for a period of 12 months following termination. The non-solicitation covenant at section 12 restricts you from soliciting Northshore's clients for the same period.
>
> We demand that you immediately:
>
> 1. Cease all competitive activities in breach of section 11 of the Agreement;
> 2. Cease all solicitation of Northshore's clients in breach of section 12; and
> 3. Confirm in writing by **April 30, 2026** that you have complied with demands (1) and (2).
>
> If we do not receive satisfactory confirmation of compliance by that date, Northshore will apply to the Supreme Court of British Columbia for injunctive relief, including an interim injunction restraining you from further breaches, together with damages, costs, and such further relief as the Court considers just. Northshore will rely on this letter as evidence of your knowledge of the breach and your refusal to comply.
>
> Our client reserves all rights and remedies available at law and in equity.
>
> We recommend you obtain independent legal advice regarding this matter without delay.
>
> Yours truly,
>
> **Brahm Dorst**
> Logica Law

## Checklist Before Sending

- [ ] Confirmed the opposing party is **not currently represented** (or you have counsel's consent to write directly)
- [ ] Tone is firm and professional — no personal attacks, no threats beyond what you can legally pursue
- [ ] Factual statements are accurate and supportable
- [ ] Demand is specific and quantified
- [ ] Deadline is a specific calendar date and is reasonable
- [ ] Consequences of non-compliance are lawful and within your client's actual intended course of action
- [ ] No legal advice given to the opposing party
- [ ] Includes recommendation to seek independent legal advice (for unrepresented parties)
- [ ] Reservation of rights included
- [ ] Key documents attached or referenced with sufficient particularity
- [ ] Letter reviewed for anything you would not want a judge to see
